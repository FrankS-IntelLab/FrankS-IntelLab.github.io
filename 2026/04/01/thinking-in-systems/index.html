On Thinking in Systems

Complicated problems have many parts. Complex problems have many interactions.

The Distinction

A car engine is complicated — thousands of parts, but each one behaves predictably. A market is complex — fewer moving pieces, but their interactions produce emergent, unpredictable behavior.

Most people apply complicated-problem thinking to complex problems. They try to enumerate all the parts and optimize each one. It doesn’t work.

What Systems Thinking Changes

You stop asking what caused this and start asking what feedback loop produced this.

You stop looking for the one lever to pull and start mapping the delays, amplifiers, and constraints in the system.

The Practical Shift

When something goes wrong, resist the first explanation. Ask:

  • What reinforces this behavior?
  • What would have to be true for this to keep happening?
  • Where is the delay between action and consequence?

The answer is rarely where you first looked.
